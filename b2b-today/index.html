The State of B2B E-Commerce

B2B e-commerce has fundamentally changed how companies buy and sell. According to Forrester, over 70% of B2B buyers prefer to research and purchase online rather than through a sales representative. The expectation is a seamless digital experience that mirrors B2C — with the added complexity of company accounts, approval workflows, contract pricing and ERP integration.

The challenge for B2B sellers is not just building a webshop — it is building a system that handles the full complexity of business-to-business commerce: customer-specific product catalogues, negotiated price lists, order approval chains, purchase order numbers, and real-time inventory from the ERP system.

B2B E-Commerce Platforms

Choosing the right platform is the foundation of a successful B2B e-commerce investment. The two dominant choices for mid-market and enterprise B2B:

Shopify Plus B2B

Shopify Plus has built a dedicated B2B commerce suite with company accounts, custom catalogues, customer-specific pricing, payment terms and wholesale storefronts — all natively integrated.

Adobe Commerce (Magento) B2B

Magento's B2B module covers shared catalogues, company hierarchies, quotes, PunchOut and requisition lists — making it the choice for complex multi-channel B2B operations.

Headless B2B Commerce

For companies with unique UX requirements, a headless architecture separates the front-end experience from the commerce engine — enabling maximum flexibility with a React or Next.js storefront.

Procurement Integration

Enterprise B2B buyers often require PunchOut integration (cXML or OCI) to purchase directly from their procurement systems — SAP Ariba, Coupa, Jaggaer or Basware.

ERP Integration: The Core of B2B Digital Commerce

A B2B webshop without ERP integration creates manual work and data inconsistency. Real-time synchronisation between the e-commerce platform and the ERP system (Business Central, SAP, Jeeves, Fortnox or Visma) is not optional — it is the foundation that makes digital B2B commerce operationally viable at scale.

Integration scope typically covers product data and pricing, customer account data, order ingestion, inventory levels and invoicing. The technical approach — direct API, middleware (iPaaS) or custom connector — depends on the ERP system and the complexity of the data model.
